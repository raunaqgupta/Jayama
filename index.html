Jayama

Shruti Sunderraman

I remember when you would piece
together patches of my torn
uniform the way you pieced
together broken affections.
The little sui and dhaaga
you kept close to the
turmeric container
(life’s first aid kit)
sewed many a severed
fabric of love.
you doctored into giving
this family,
our clothes
and my patience a longer life.

When I step into the
precipice of a world,
i promise
you wouldn’t recognise,
I look for your hand
over my shoulder
the same way I
did on my bicycle rides.
You’d run after me
in an ombodhu gajam
whose graceful drapes
and firm knots
could give governments
lessons in efficiency.
I’d notice parents in the garden
laugh at you
I’d notice how you only
had eyes for me;
the world’s condescension
never qualified to
take up space
and soil in your backyard.

As I gobble down this
500 rupee pasta,
I’d remember the time
when food wasn’t valued in
money but
in the willingness to
share.
We’d surround you in a
semi-circle till
you’d drop a spoon
of thair saadam into
eager palms.
You’d see
our barely contained hunger
and ignore it -
we had to wait our turns,
we had to wait
for the spoon to return
we had to learn to share
what was never ours to keep.
I look for you, paati,
in the centre of
my semi-circle of
hungry ambitions,
not knowing when
to extend my palm
and when
to await my turn.

With you,
I spent my childhood in
careless abandon,
but you left before
you could tell me when
it stops -
I now spend
my adulthood letting go,
without the grace
of your kindness.
As I try to find my voice
in a world determined
to drown it,
I listen to the
distant echoes of
your lessons.
Amid false leaders,
a reckless people,
apathy
and fear,
I look for the fortitude
with which you
lifted us from the poverty
of our hearts
in a one-room-kitchen.

I look for you
at the edge of Decision,
hoping my voice
inherited your assertion.
I reminisce
how you taught me
“properly” is what I make of it,
so I sit as I please.
I can never tuck in my saree’s drapes
the way you did,
but i drape your values
in them tight -
each lesson in each fold
held close to my navel.
Like you, paati,
I am any woman I want to be
Any person I want to become.
A potential wasted
or a privilege gained,
I hope to find them a corner
in your backyard
and water them
till their green tendons,
become poems to you.
